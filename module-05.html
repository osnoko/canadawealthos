CRA Self-Employment Rules — What Every Canadian Side Hustler Must Know

The moment you earn income outside an employer, you're self-employed in the eyes of the CRA. The rules aren't complicated — but ignoring them is expensive.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 05
📋 Know the Rules

7.4 million Canadians now run a side hustle. Most have no idea how the CRA views their income, what they must report, or what they can deduct. This lesson fixes that.

Are You Self-Employed? The CRA Definition

The CRA considers you self-employed if you earn income from any activity where you set your own hours, determine your own methods, use your own tools, and take on financial risk. This includes freelancing, gig work (Uber, DoorDash, TaskRabbit), selling online (Etsy, Shopify, eBay), content creation (YouTube, sponsorships, Patreon), Airbnb rental income, and any service provided for payment as a non-employee.

⚠️ There Is No Minimum Threshold

Canada has no minimum self-employment income before you must report. If you earn $1 in self-employment income, it must be reported on your T1 using Form T2125. The CRA receives payment data from PayPal, Stripe, Amazon, Etsy, and gig platforms. Unreported income is a CRA audit trigger.

The CPP Double Hit

As an employee, your employer matches your CPP contributions. As a self-employed person, you pay both the employee and employer portions — effectively doubling your rate to 11.9% on net self-employment income up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings.

💡 The Tax Reserve Rule

Set aside 28–30% of every self-employment payment in a separate account the day you receive it. This covers income tax at your marginal rate plus the CPP double contribution. Never touch this account until tax time. This one habit eliminates the single biggest financial shock self-employed Canadians face.

Form T2125 — Your Business Tax Summary

All self-employment income and expenses are reported on Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities), filed as part of your annual T1 return. It captures gross revenue, all deductible expenses, net income, home office use, and vehicle expenses. Net income from T2125 flows to your T1 and is subject to both income tax and CPP — which is exactly why every deduction you claim reduces both simultaneously.

🍁 Sole Proprietor vs. Incorporation

You don't need to incorporate to be self-employed. You can operate as a sole proprietor under your legal name and report income on T2125 — no registration needed. If you want to use a business name, register it with ServiceOntario for $60. Incorporating typically makes financial sense only when annual net profit exceeds $50,000–$100,000 and you want to access the small business tax rate of 12.2% in Ontario.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ All self-employment income must be reported in Canada — there is no minimum threshold
  • ✦ Self-employed Canadians pay both sides of CPP (11.9%) — plan for this from day one
  • ✦ Set aside 28–30% of every payment in a separate tax reserve account immediately
  • ✦ Form T2125 captures income and deductions — net income is what you're taxed on
  • ✦ Every dollar deducted reduces both income tax AND CPP contributions simultaneously

HST Registration — The $30,000 Rule Canadian Side Hustlers Must Know

Missing the HST registration threshold is a CRA compliance issue. Registering voluntarily before the threshold can save you money. Here's the full picture.

⏱ ~7 min read
📘 Module 05
🧾 The $30K Threshold

Most new self-employed Canadians don't think about HST until they're forced to register. By then, they may have missed the threshold, charged clients without a valid BN, or failed to collect when required.

The $30,000 Rule

You must register for a GST/HST account once your total taxable revenues exceed $30,000 in any single calendar quarter, or over four consecutive calendar quarters. Once you hit this threshold, register within 29 days and begin charging HST immediately.

⚠️ The Threshold Is Cumulative

The $30,000 threshold counts ALL your business activities combined — freelancing, Etsy, Uber, Airbnb. Many multi-income Canadians hit the threshold faster than expected without realizing it. Failing to register on time means the CRA can hold you responsible for the HST you should have collected — retroactively.

Why Register Voluntarily Before $30,000?

Voluntary registration makes sense when:

  • Your clients are businesses — B2B clients claim the HST you charge as an Input Tax Credit (ITC), so it costs them nothing. Many actually prefer HST-registered suppliers
  • You have significant business expenses — once registered, you claim ITCs on HST paid for all business purchases, often generating a net refund
  • You're approaching $30K quickly — register early to avoid the compliance scramble
💡 The Quick Method

If annual revenues are under $400,000, you may qualify for the HST Quick Method — remit a flat percentage of gross revenue (4.4% for Ontario service businesses) instead of tracking every ITC individually. For businesses with few expenses, this simplifies HST filing dramatically and sometimes results in keeping a small portion of the HST collected.

🍁 How to Register

Register online through CRA My Business Account at canada.ca — free, takes 10 minutes, processes in about 5 business days. You'll receive a Business Number (BN) ending in RT0001. Once registered: charge applicable HST on all taxable sales, show your BN on invoices, file quarterly or annual HST returns, and remit net HST (collected minus ITCs) to the CRA.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Register for HST once combined revenues from all business activities exceed $30,000
  • ✦ The threshold is cumulative across all income streams — not per business
  • ✦ Voluntary registration before $30K makes sense if you have significant business expenses
  • ✦ Ontario rate is 13% HST — charge this on all taxable services and products
  • ✦ The Quick Method simplifies HST filing for service businesses under $400K revenue
⚡ This Week's Action Items
Add up all your self-employment revenue streams for the past 12 months — are you approaching $30,000?
If over $30K, register at canada.ca/cra-my-business-account immediately
Research the HST Quick Method and whether it applies to your business type
Update your invoice template to include your BN and HST amount as a separate line item

Top T2125 Deductions — Keep More of What You Earn

CRA allows you to deduct all reasonable expenses incurred to earn self-employment income. Most Canadians claim 30–50% less than they're entitled to. Here's the full list.

⏱ ~9 min read
📘 Module 05
💰 Keep More of What You Earn

The CRA allows you to deduct any expense that is "reasonable" and incurred to earn business income. The key word is reasonable — the expense must have a genuine business purpose, and if it has both personal and business use, only the business portion is deductible.

The Major T2125 Deduction Categories

1. Home Office (Business Use of Home)

If you work from home, you can deduct a portion of home expenses proportional to the space used exclusively for business. Calculate your home office percentage:

Formula: (Office square footage ÷ Total home square footage) × 100 = Business use %

Apply this percentage to: rent or mortgage interest, property tax, heat, electricity, home insurance, and internet.

💡 The Dedicated Space Rule

The CRA requires the home office space be used exclusively and regularly for business. A kitchen table doesn't qualify. A spare bedroom used only as a home office does. Measure it accurately — the CRA can request floor plans during an audit. A 150 sq ft office in a 1,200 sq ft home = 12.5% deductible on all eligible home expenses.

2. Vehicle Expenses

If you use a vehicle for business (client visits, supply runs, delivery, gig driving), you can deduct the business-use percentage of all vehicle costs: gas, insurance, maintenance, licensing, and either lease payments or Capital Cost Allowance (depreciation) on a purchased vehicle.

Key requirement: Keep a mileage log showing date, destination, business purpose, and km driven for every business trip. The CRA considers this the most important documentation for vehicle deductions.

3. Office Supplies & Equipment

Deductible immediately: pens, paper, printer ink, postage, packaging materials, small tools under $500. Equipment over $500 (laptop, camera, printer) is typically depreciated over time using Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) — though there are immediate expensing rules that may allow 100% deduction in year one for eligible equipment.

4. Phone & Internet

The business-use portion of your phone and internet bills is fully deductible. If you use your phone 60% for business, deduct 60% of your monthly bill. Keep 2–3 months of bills as documentation. A dedicated business line is 100% deductible.

5. Professional Services

Fully deductible: accountant fees for business tax preparation, lawyer fees for business contracts, bookkeeper fees. Your personal tax return preparation is not deductible — but the portion attributable to your T2125 is.

6. Advertising & Marketing

Fully deductible: website hosting, domain registration, social media advertising, Google Ads, business cards, logo design, photography for business use, CanadaWealthOS and similar educational courses directly related to your business.

7. Software & Subscriptions

Any software used for business is deductible: accounting software (QuickBooks, Wave), design tools (Canva Pro, Adobe), project management (Notion, Asana), cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox), video editing tools (CapCut Pro, DaVinci). Keep receipts and document the business purpose for each subscription.

8. Meals & Entertainment

Only 50% deductible — and only for meals with clients or business partners where business is discussed. Personal meals are never deductible. Keep receipts with the business purpose and attendees written on the back.

🍁 The Receipt Retention Rule

The CRA requires you to keep all business receipts and records for 6 years from the end of the tax year they relate to. Digital copies are accepted — scan or photograph every receipt and store in a dedicated folder (Google Drive, Dropbox). Apps like Wave and Dext make receipt capture effortless. Missing receipts during an audit = denied deductions.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Home office deduction = % of home expenses matching your office's share of total floor space
  • ✦ Vehicle deductions require a mileage log — this is the CRA's most requested documentation
  • ✦ Phone, internet, software, and marketing expenses are 100% deductible (business portion)
  • ✦ Meals and entertainment are only 50% deductible — and only with genuine business purpose
  • ✦ Keep all receipts digitally for 6 years — missing receipts = denied deductions in an audit

Side Hustle Ideas Canada — What Actually Pays in 2026

Not all side hustles are equal. Some scale. Some don't. Some work in Hamilton. Some don't work anywhere. Here's the honest breakdown for Canadians in 2026.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 05
💡 What Pays in 2026

A side hustle that generates $500–$1,500/month in Ontario doesn't just add spending money. Applied to debt payoff or TFSA contributions, it becomes one of the most powerful financial tools available to working Canadians.

Tier 1 — High Income, Scalable

These hustles can replace a full-time income if developed over 12–24 months:

  • Freelance tech (web dev, UX, cybersecurity consulting) — $50–$150/hr. Remote, high demand. CompTIA certifications or a portfolio on GitHub unlocks this market immediately
  • Digital products — ebooks, templates, courses, prompt packs. Create once, sell forever. Low overhead, 90%+ margins, Stripe + Gumroad make payments instant
  • Content creation (YouTube, TikTok, newsletters) — slow to monetize but scales to $5K–$50K+/month with the right niche. Canadian finance, immigration, and career content has strong demand
  • Consulting/coaching — leverages existing professional expertise. A cybersecurity professional can charge $100–$200/hr for SMB security audits

Tier 2 — Reliable Income, Lower Ceiling

Solid $500–$2,000/month potential with consistent effort:

  • Gig delivery (DoorDash, Uber Eats, Instacart) — $18–$25/hr effective in Ontario after expenses. Flexible, immediate income. Track mileage obsessively for deductions
  • Print-on-demand (Printify + Etsy/Shopify) — no inventory, global reach. Works well for niche communities (faith-based, sports, cultural identity). Margins 20–40%
  • Tutoring and test prep — $30–$80/hr for academic tutoring. ESL tutoring for newcomers to Canada pays $25–$45/hr and is in high demand in Hamilton and GTA
  • Handyman / skilled trades — Ontario has a shortage of skilled tradespeople. Licensed electricians, plumbers, and HVAC techs command $80–$120/hr for side work

Tier 3 — Supplemental Income

Good for $100–$500/month with minimal time investment:

  • Selling unused items — Kijiji, Facebook Marketplace, eBay. A one-time purge of a Canadian home generates $500–$2,000 on average
  • Survey and research panels — Leger, Angus Reid, Respondent.io. $10–$50/hr for qualified participants. Limited availability but zero overhead
  • Renting assets — car on Turo, tools on Loanables, parking space on SpotHero or Rover. Passive once set up
🍁 The Two-Account Protection System

Never mix side hustle money with personal funds. Open two dedicated accounts: (1) a business chequing account where all income lands and all business expenses are paid from, and (2) a tax reserve savings account where 28% of every deposit is immediately transferred. This system makes bookkeeping simple, makes taxes predictable, and protects you if the CRA ever requests business records — your personal account never appears in the business record.

The $1,000/Month Challenge

An extra $1,000/month from a side hustle — after setting aside 28% for taxes ($280) — leaves $720 net. Applied strategically:

  • $720/month into a TFSA at 8% annual return = $1.06M after 30 years
  • $720/month toward a $20,000 debt at 22% interest = debt-free in 28 months instead of 18 years on minimums
  • $720/month into an emergency fund = 3-month fund in 12 months

The side hustle isn't the strategy. What you do with the side hustle income is the strategy.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Tier 1 hustles (freelance tech, digital products, content) can replace full-time income within 24 months
  • ✦ Print-on-demand, gig delivery, and tutoring offer reliable $500–$2K/month with consistent effort
  • ✦ The two-account system (business chequing + tax reserve) protects both your books and your sanity
  • ✦ $1,000/month net side hustle income into a TFSA = $1M+ after 30 years
  • ✦ The hustle creates the income — the system determines whether it builds wealth
