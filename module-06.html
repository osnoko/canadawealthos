Your Canadian FI Number — The Exact Amount You Need to Retire

Financial Independence isn't a vague dream. It's a specific dollar number — and once you know yours, every financial decision you make has a target.

⏱ ~9 min read
📘 Module 06
🎯 Calculate Your Target

Financial Independence means your investment portfolio generates enough passive income to cover your living expenses indefinitely — without needing to work. The number that makes this possible is your FI number. Every Canadian's is different. Here's how to calculate yours.

The FI Number Formula

FI Number = Annual Expenses × 25

This formula comes directly from the 4% Rule (covered in Lesson 2), which shows that a portfolio can sustainably withdraw 4% per year in perpetuity. Multiplying your annual expenses by 25 gives you the portfolio size needed to fund those expenses at 4% annually.

Calculating Your Canadian Annual Expenses

Your FI number is only as accurate as your expense estimate. Use these Canadian-specific categories:

🍁 Canadian FI Numbers by Lifestyle

A frugal Canadian living on $40,000/year needs a $1,000,000 portfolio. A comfortable lifestyle at $60,000/year needs $1,500,000. A high-income lifestyle at $100,000/year needs $2,500,000. Remember: these numbers assume CPP and OAS are NOT counted — when you factor in CPP ($10,000–$15,000/year) and OAS ($8,000–$9,000/year), your portfolio requirement drops by $250,000–$600,000.

FI Number vs. FIRE Number

FI (Financial Independence) means you could stop working. FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) means you actually do. Most Canadians pursue FI as the goal — the freedom to choose work, not the obligation to work — and continue some form of income-generating activity they enjoy.

Types of FIRE relevant to Canadians:

  • Lean FIRE — $600K–$900K portfolio, $25,000–$35,000/year spending, very frugal lifestyle
  • Regular FIRE — $1M–$1.5M portfolio, $40,000–$60,000/year, moderate lifestyle
  • Fat FIRE — $2.5M+ portfolio, $80,000–$100,000+/year, no lifestyle compromises
  • Barista FIRE — smaller portfolio + part-time income to cover the gap. Popular in Canada due to the healthcare coverage question being less acute (we have provincial health coverage)
📊 Canada's Healthcare Advantage for FIRE

One of the biggest barriers to early retirement in the US is losing employer-sponsored health insurance. In Canada, provincial health coverage continues regardless of employment status. This makes early retirement significantly more achievable for Canadians — you don't need to budget an extra $15,000–$25,000/year (US average) for private health insurance. This alone reduces the Canadian FI number by $375,000–$625,000 compared to an equivalent American pursuing the same lifestyle.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Your FI Number = Annual Expenses × 25 — a specific, calculable target
  • ✦ CPP and OAS reduce your required portfolio by $250,000–$600,000 depending on your benefit amounts
  • ✦ Canada's universal healthcare eliminates a major FIRE cost that burdens American retirees
  • ✦ FI means freedom to choose work — FIRE means choosing to stop. Both require the same number
  • ✦ Knowing your number transforms every financial decision — it becomes your north star

The 4% Rule in Canada — What It Really Means for Canadian Retirees

The 4% rule is the most cited retirement guideline in the world. But it was built on US data. Here's what changes — and what doesn't — for Canadians.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 06
📊 Canadian Adjustments

The 4% Rule comes from the Trinity Study (1998), which analyzed US stock and bond portfolio performance from 1926–1995. It concluded that withdrawing 4% of your initial portfolio annually — adjusted for inflation — had a 95%+ success rate over 30 years.

For Canadians, the rule holds — with some important adjustments.

How the 4% Rule Works

Year 1: You retire with $1,000,000. You withdraw $40,000 (4%). Year 2: You withdraw $40,000 × inflation (say 2.5%) = $41,000. Year 3: $41,000 × inflation. And so on — the withdrawal grows with inflation, but the portfolio (invested in equities and bonds) continues to grow alongside it.

The math works because diversified equity portfolios have historically returned 7–10% annually. Even after a 4% withdrawal and 2–3% inflation adjustment, the portfolio continues compounding.

🍁 The Canadian Adjustments

Currency: Canadian stocks slightly underperformed US stocks historically. A globally diversified ETF (XEQT) solves this — you own the entire world market, not just Canada. CPP/OAS: These government benefits reduce the amount you need to withdraw from your portfolio, effectively making the 4% rule more conservative — meaning safer. A Canadian with $1,000/month CPP and $750/month OAS withdraws $21,000/year less from their portfolio, dramatically extending its life. Healthcare: No private insurance cost in retirement. Result: The 4% rule is slightly more achievable for Canadians than Americans, all else being equal.

Safe Withdrawal Rate by Scenario

Sequence of Returns Risk — The Biggest Threat

The 4% rule's main enemy isn't average returns — it's bad returns early in retirement. If markets drop 30% in your first two years of retirement and you're withdrawing 4%, your portfolio may not recover even if markets rebound strongly afterward.

Canadian mitigation strategies:

  • The Cash Buffer — keep 1–2 years of expenses in HISA. Draw from this during down markets so you're not selling equities at a loss
  • The Flexible Withdrawal — reduce withdrawals by 10–20% in market downturns; return to normal in recoveries
  • The Bond Tent — hold slightly more bonds (40–50%) in the 5 years before and after retirement, then gradually shift back to equity-heavy as the sequence-of-returns risk window passes
🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ The 4% rule works for Canadians — global diversification via XEQT solves the Canadian equity underperformance issue
  • ✦ CPP and OAS effectively boost your sustainable withdrawal rate by reducing portfolio dependence
  • ✦ Earlier retirement requires a more conservative rate (3–3.5%) due to longer time horizons
  • ✦ Sequence of returns risk — bad markets early in retirement — is the primary threat to manage
  • ✦ A cash buffer of 1–2 years of expenses protects against forced equity selling during downturns

CPP & OAS Strategy — Maximizing Canada's Government Retirement Income

CPP and OAS are the two pillars of Canada's public pension system. Most Canadians leave tens of thousands on the table by claiming at the wrong time. Here's the optimal strategy.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 06
🏛️ Maximize Government Income

CPP and OAS are inflation-indexed, government-guaranteed income streams. For most Canadians, they represent $500,000–$800,000 in present-value retirement income. Optimizing when and how you claim them is one of the highest-leverage retirement decisions you'll ever make.

Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

CPP replaces a portion of your pre-retirement income based on how much you contributed and for how long. The 2026 maximum CPP at age 65 is approximately $1,364/month — but the average Canadian receives around $700–$800/month because most don't max out contributions over a 39-year career.

💡 The Break-Even Analysis

Deferring CPP from 65 to 70 costs you 5 years of payments (~$54,900) but pays you $321/month more forever. The break-even age — when deferred payments catch up — is approximately age 82–83. Canadians who live past 83 (average life expectancy is 83 for men, 85 for women) are financially better off deferring to 70. If your health suggests a shorter lifespan, claiming at 65 or earlier may be optimal.

Old Age Security (OAS)

OAS is a universal government pension paid to Canadians 65+ who have lived in Canada for at least 10 years after age 18. The 2026 maximum OAS is approximately $727/month at age 65 (indexed quarterly to inflation).

Key OAS facts:

  • No contribution required — unlike CPP, OAS doesn't depend on work history
  • Residency-based — 40 years of Canadian residency after 18 = full OAS; partial for fewer years
  • Deferral bonus — like CPP, you can defer OAS up to age 70 for a 36% permanent increase
  • Clawback threshold — if your net income exceeds ~$90,997 (2026), OAS is clawed back at 15 cents per dollar above the threshold
🍁 The OAS Clawback Strategy

If your retirement income (RRSP withdrawals, pension, investment income) pushes you above the OAS clawback threshold, use these strategies to stay below: withdraw from TFSA instead of RRSP (TFSA income is not counted), split pension income with a spouse, manage RRSP-to-RRIF conversions carefully, and consider Prescribed Rate Loans to shift investment income to a lower-income spouse. The clawback affects higher-income retirees — but with planning, it's largely avoidable.

Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS)

If your retirement income is below approximately $21,000/year (single person, 2026), you may qualify for the Guaranteed Income Supplement — an additional tax-free monthly payment up to $1,054/month. GIS is income-tested and reduces as income rises. If you're pursuing a lean FIRE or early retirement with minimal RRSP withdrawals, GIS eligibility in later years can significantly boost your income floor.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ CPP at 70 pays 42% more than at 65 — Canadians who live past 82–83 are better off deferring
  • ✦ OAS is universal and inflation-indexed — deferring to 70 adds 36% permanently
  • ✦ TFSA withdrawals don't count as income — use them strategically to avoid the OAS clawback
  • ✦ GIS provides additional tax-free income for low-income retirees — plan RRSP withdrawals carefully if this applies
  • ✦ Combined CPP + OAS can provide $1,500–$2,000/month — reducing your portfolio withdrawal requirement significantly

The 5 Financial Stages of a Canadian Wealth Journey

  1. Stage 1 — Stability: Emergency fund (3–6 months), basic budget, CRA My Account set up, debt minimum payments automated
  2. Stage 2 — Defence: High-interest debt eliminated, TFSA opened and funded, FHSA opened (if applicable), HST registered (if self-employed)
  3. Stage 3 — Offence: Maxing TFSA annually, RRSP contributions underway, side hustle income flowing, net worth growing
  4. Stage 4 — Acceleration: All registered accounts maximized, non-registered investing started, net worth doubling every 7–10 years via compound growth
  5. Stage 5 — Independence: Portfolio at FI number, CPP/OAS strategy in place, work becomes optional

The Canadian Wealth Acceleration Formula

The three variables that determine how fast you reach FI:

Wealth Speed = (Income − Expenses) × Investment Return Rate

You control two of the three variables. Investment return rate is largely fixed by the market (7–9% long term for a diversified ETF portfolio). But income and expenses are completely within your control. The highest-leverage actions:

  • Increase income — career growth, side hustle, skill development (cybersecurity, tech, trades)
  • Decrease expenses — the budget audit (Module 01) finds money you didn't know you were losing
  • Close the gap — every $500/month increase in the gap between income and expenses cuts your time to FI by years
