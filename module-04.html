ETFs vs. Mutual Funds — The Fee Truth Canadian Banks Don't Advertise

Canada has some of the highest mutual fund fees in the world. This lesson reveals exactly what you're paying — and why switching to ETFs could add hundreds of thousands to your retirement.

⏱ ~9 min read
📘 Module 04
📊 The Fee Truth

Canada has a problem. Our mutual fund Management Expense Ratios (MERs) average 2.0–2.5% annually — among the highest in the developed world. The US average is 0.5%. Australia: 0.8%. The UK: 0.9%.

That gap doesn't sound dramatic. Over 30 years, it's the difference between retiring comfortably and not retiring at all.

What Is an MER and Why Does It Destroy Wealth?

The Management Expense Ratio is the annual fee charged by a fund to cover management, administration, and distribution costs. It's expressed as a percentage of your invested assets — and it's deducted automatically, so you never see it leave your account. That invisibility is by design.

That $311,000 gap is entirely caused by fees. Same market returns. Same time period. The only variable is what the fund charges you every year.

⚠️ The Trailer Fee System

Until recently, Canadian financial advisors received "trailer fees" — ongoing annual commissions paid by mutual fund companies for keeping client money in their funds. These fees (0.5–1% annually) were paid by the fund's MER, which you paid. Many advisors recommended higher-fee funds simply because they paid higher trailers. While regulators have tightened rules, conflicts of interest still exist. Always ask: "How are you compensated for recommending this product?"

ETFs: The Structural Advantage

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) are investment funds that trade on a stock exchange like a share. They typically track an index — a basket of hundreds or thousands of stocks — passively, without active management. This is why they cost almost nothing to run.

Key structural advantages over mutual funds:

  • Lower MER — typically 0.10–0.25% vs. 1.5–2.5% for actively managed mutual funds
  • Intraday trading — ETFs trade throughout the day; mutual funds price once daily at market close
  • Tax efficiency — ETFs generate fewer taxable capital gain distributions than mutual funds
  • Transparency — ETF holdings are published daily; mutual funds often only quarterly
  • No minimums — buy one share at a time; mutual funds often require $500–$5,000 minimum purchases
📊 The Active Management Myth

Every year, SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) publishes data on how actively managed funds perform versus their benchmark index. The 2024 report shows that over a 15-year period, over 90% of Canadian actively managed equity funds underperformed their benchmark index after fees. You pay 10x more in fees for worse performance. The data is unambiguous.

The Exception: When Mutual Funds Still Make Sense

Despite the fee disadvantage, mutual funds can still be appropriate in specific situations:

  • Group RRSPs — employer-sponsored plans often only offer mutual funds; still contribute for the employer match
  • Robo-advisors — platforms like Wealthsimple Invest use ETF portfolios but charge a platform fee (0.4–0.5%); slightly higher than DIY but fully automated
  • Behavioural guardrails — if you'll panic-sell during a market drop, a locked-in mutual fund structure may prevent costly mistakes
🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Canadian mutual fund MERs (2–2.5%) are among the highest globally — ETFs average 0.10–0.25%
  • ✦ The fee gap creates a $300,000+ wealth difference on $100,000 invested over 30 years
  • ✦ Over 90% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over 15 years
  • ✦ ETFs offer structural advantages: lower cost, tax efficiency, transparency, and no minimums
  • ✦ Group RRSPs with employer match are still valuable even if they only offer mutual funds

If you use Questrade and want to buy US-listed ETFs without paying 1.99% FX fees, Google "Norbert's Gambit Questrade." The process involves buying a Canada-listed ETF (like DLR) and journaling it to the US side, then selling. It's a 2-3 day process that cuts currency conversion costs from ~2% to ~0.1%. Worth it on conversions over $5,000.

🍁 The CDIC Protection Note

Both Wealthsimple Trade and Questrade are members of the Canadian Investor Protection Fund (CIPF), which protects eligible investment accounts up to $1,000,000 per account category if the brokerage becomes insolvent. This is separate from CDIC protection (which covers bank deposits). Your ETF holdings are protected at both platforms.

XEQT & XBAL — The One-Fund Canadian Portfolio Strategy

You don't need 12 ETFs to build a well-diversified Canadian portfolio. Two funds — XEQT and XBAL — cover the entire world market. Here's how to choose between them and why they work.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 04
📈 The One-Fund Portfolio

One of the biggest myths in investing is that building wealth requires complexity — a portfolio of 10–15 carefully selected funds rebalanced quarterly. The evidence says otherwise.

A single all-in-one ETF, held consistently and added to regularly, outperforms most actively managed multi-fund portfolios over any 20-year period. Here's the Canadian version.

What Is XEQT?

XEQT (iShares Core Equity ETF Portfolio) is a single fund that holds approximately 9,000 global stocks across Canada, the US, international developed markets, and emerging markets. It's 100% equity — meaning no bonds — and automatically rebalances itself.

XEQT vs XBAL — The Decision

The standard rule of thumb: your bond allocation should roughly equal your age. A 30-year-old might hold 30% bonds (closer to XBAL), while a 25-year-old with a 40-year horizon holds 100% equity (XEQT).

But the modern approach for Canadians investing in a TFSA or RRSP with a 20+ year horizon: XEQT all the way. Here's why:

  • You have decades to ride out market volatility
  • Your TFSA/RRSP contributions are long-term — not money you need next year
  • The historical return difference (9.5% vs 7.2%) compounds to a massive wealth gap over 30 years
  • Bonds provide diversification but also drag returns in strong equity markets
💡 The 2024 Market Context

In 2022, when interest rates spiked, bonds fell alongside stocks — their traditional diversification benefit temporarily disappeared. XBAL investors saw losses in both their equity and bond allocations simultaneously. For long-term investors under 50, this event reinforced the case for XEQT over XBAL during the accumulation phase.

The Dollar-Cost Averaging Strategy

The most powerful strategy with XEQT or XBAL isn't timing the market — it's consistent contributions regardless of market conditions.

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) means buying a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals (monthly or biweekly with your paycheque). This automatically buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

📊 DCA vs Lump Sum — The Canadian Reality

Studies show lump-sum investing outperforms DCA approximately 68% of the time when a large amount is available. But most Canadians don't have $20,000 available at once — they have $500/month from their paycheque. For regular paycheque investors, DCA is not just the practical choice — it's the psychologically sustainable one. Automatic biweekly contributions to XEQT, held for 30 years, is the foundation of most Canadian retirement wealth.

  • ✦ Both automatically rebalance — no manual intervention needed after purchase
  • ✦ Dollar-cost averaging with monthly paycheque contributions is the sustainable strategy for most Canadians

DRIP Investing — How Reinvested Dividends Build Passive Wealth

The Dividend Reinvestment Plan is one of the most underused wealth-building tools available to Canadian investors. Here's how it works — and why enabling it is one of the highest-leverage decisions you can make.

⏱ ~7 min read
📘 Module 04
💰 Dividends Reinvested

When XEQT or any dividend-paying ETF distributes income, you have two choices: take the cash, or reinvest it automatically into more shares. The second option — the DRIP — is the difference between good wealth-building and exceptional wealth-building.

How DRIP Works

A Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP) automatically uses dividend and distribution payments to purchase additional shares of the same fund. Instead of receiving $47 in distributions and having it sit in cash, it immediately buys more XEQT shares — which then generate their own distributions — which buy more shares — in a continuously compounding loop.

DRIP Inside a TFSA — The Ultimate Setup

DRIP is powerful in any account. Inside a TFSA, it's extraordinary — because:

  • Dividends and distributions are completely tax-free
  • Reinvested distributions generate more tax-free distributions
  • Every compounding cycle happens with zero CRA involvement
  • Over 30+ years, the DRIP within a TFSA creates a compounding engine that grows exponentially faster than a taxable account
📊 The 40-Year DRIP Illustration

An investor who puts $7,000/year into XEQT inside a TFSA with DRIP enabled, starting at age 25, accumulates approximately $1.85M by age 65 at a 9% average annual return. The same investor without DRIP (taking distributions as cash) accumulates approximately $1.52M. The DRIP creates an additional $330,000 — entirely from reinvesting distributions automatically. No extra work. No extra contributions. Just the compounding of dividends.

How to Enable DRIP on Wealthsimple and Questrade

Wealthsimple Trade: Go to your account settings → Dividend Reinvestment → toggle on. Distributions from ETFs will automatically purchase fractional shares (Wealthsimple supports fractional shares, which means every cent of distribution buys more shares immediately).

Questrade: DRIP is available but requires a phone call or in-app request to enable per-security. Worth the 5-minute call for long-term holdings.

💡 Fractional Shares Matter

Wealthsimple Trade supports fractional share purchases with DRIP, meaning a $47.32 distribution buys exactly $47.32 worth of XEQT — no cash left sitting idle. Questrade's DRIP only works when the distribution is large enough to buy a full share. For smaller accounts, Wealthsimple's fractional DRIP is more efficient.

The Canadian Dividend Tax Credit — For Non-Registered Accounts

If you're investing in a non-registered (taxable) account — meaning you've maxed your TFSA and RRSP — Canadian eligible dividends receive preferential tax treatment through the Dividend Tax Credit (DTC).

Eligible Canadian dividends are "grossed up" by 38% on your tax return, then you receive a federal DTC of 15.02% of the grossed-up amount. For many investors in lower tax brackets, Canadian eligible dividends are taxed at near-zero — sometimes negative — effective rates. This makes Canadian dividend ETFs (like XDV or CDZ) compelling in non-registered accounts.
