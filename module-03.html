OSAP Repayment — The Strategy Most Graduates Get Wrong

Your student loan is not just a bill. It's a financial instrument — and knowing how to manage it correctly can save you thousands and accelerate your path to wealth.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 03
🎓 Student Debt Strategy

Over 1.7 million Canadians carry OSAP debt. The average Ontario graduate owes approximately $28,000. And most of them have no plan beyond making the minimum payment and hoping it goes away.

That's an expensive mistake. Here's what they don't teach you at convocation.

How OSAP Works After Graduation

When you graduate — or leave school — OSAP gives you a 6-month non-repayment period. During this time, you don't have to make payments. But interest does accumulate on the federal portion of your loan.

After those 6 months, you enter repayment. Your loan is divided into two parts:

  • Federal portion — administered by the National Student Loans Service Centre (NSLSC)
  • Ontario provincial portion — administered separately, but now interest-free since 2023
📊 2023 Policy Win

As of November 2023, the federal government eliminated interest on the federal portion of OSAP loans entirely. This is a massive change — it means your OSAP balance no longer grows through interest. Every dollar you pay goes directly to principal reduction. If you graduated before this change, your remaining balance is still subject to the old terms — check your NSLSC account.

The Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)

If your income is low after graduation, you may qualify for the Repayment Assistance Plan. RAP caps your monthly payment at a percentage of your income — and if your income is below a certain threshold, your payment could be $0.

RAP thresholds for a single person (2025):

Annual IncomeMonthly Payment CapStrategy
Under $25,000$0/monthApply for RAP immediately

💡 The RAP Strategy

If you qualify for RAP and your loan is now interest-free, there's a case for making minimum payments while investing the difference. An OSAP loan at 0% interest is the cheapest money you'll ever have. Putting extra cash into a TFSA earning 7–8% annually beats paying down a 0% loan every time — mathematically.

OSAP vs. Other Debts — The Priority Order

Not all debt is created equal. Here's how to rank your debts for payoff priority when OSAP is in the mix:

  1. Credit cards (19–29% interest) — Kill these first, always
  2. Payday loans (300–600% effective APR) — Emergency — eliminate immediately
  3. Personal loans (8–18%) — High priority after credit cards
  4. Car loans (4–9%) — Medium priority
  5. OSAP (now 0% federal / 0% Ontario) — Lowest priority — invest instead
  6. Mortgage (4–6%) — Consider investing in TFSA/RRSP before extra mortgage payments
⚠️ The OSAP Default Risk

Missing OSAP payments has serious consequences: your credit score drops significantly, the government can garnish your wages without a court order, and your tax refunds can be intercepted. Always make the minimum payment — even if you're also aggressively paying other debts. If you can't afford the minimum, call NSLSC and apply for RAP before you miss a payment.

The OSAP Interest Tax Credit

If you do have an older OSAP loan that still carries interest, remember: student loan interest is tax-deductible at the federal level. You can claim interest paid on eligible student loans on line 31900 of your T1 return. The credit is worth approximately 15–20% of the interest paid, depending on your province.

Note: You can carry forward unclaimed interest credits for up to 5 years. Check your previous returns if you haven't been claiming this.

🍁 The Ontario Advantage

Ontario's OSAP is means-tested more aggressively than other provinces, meaning lower-income students receive more grant funding (non-repayable) versus loan funding. If you're still in school or have a family member entering post-secondary, always apply for OSAP regardless of expected income — the grant assessment may surprise you. Grants don't need to be repaid and don't appear on your debt ledger.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Federal OSAP loans are now interest-free — every payment reduces principal directly
  • ✦ RAP can reduce or eliminate payments when income is low — always apply rather than defaulting
  • ✦ A 0% OSAP loan is the cheapest debt you'll ever have — prioritize higher-interest debts first
  • ✦ Mathematically, investing in a TFSA at 7–8% beats paying extra on a 0% OSAP loan
  • ✦ Student loan interest (if applicable) is tax-deductible — claim it on line 31900

Avalanche vs. Snowball — The Debt Payoff Math Every Canadian Needs

Two proven methods for eliminating multiple debts. One saves you more money. One keeps you more motivated. Here's how to decide — and how to build your personal payoff stack.

⏱ ~9 min read
📘 Module 03
💳 The Payoff Math

If you carry more than one debt — a credit card, a car loan, a line of credit, and an OSAP balance — you face a strategic decision: which one do you attack first?

There are two dominant frameworks. Here's the breakdown.

The Avalanche Method

The avalanche method is mathematically optimal. You order your debts by interest rate, highest to lowest — then throw every extra dollar at the highest-rate debt while making minimum payments on everything else.

Once the top debt is gone, you roll that entire payment to the next highest-rate debt. Your monthly payment amount stays the same, but progressively more of it attacks principal.

📊 The Avalanche in Numbers

On a $20,000 debt portfolio (credit card at 22%, car loan at 7%, OSAP at 0%), the avalanche method saves an average of $1,800–$3,400 in interest versus the snowball method, and eliminates the portfolio 4–8 months faster. The gap widens significantly with higher balances and interest rates.

The Snowball Method

The snowball method is psychologically optimal. You order your debts by balance, smallest to largest — regardless of interest rate — and attack the smallest balance first.

The logic: eliminate a debt completely, feel the win, build momentum. Research shows this method produces better long-term adherence for people who struggle with motivation or have tried and failed to pay down debt before.

Building Your Canadian Debt Stack

Here's how to set up your payoff plan in 4 steps:

  1. List every debt — balance, interest rate, minimum payment
  2. Choose your method — avalanche (more savings) or snowball (more momentum)
  3. Calculate your "extra payment" — total income minus expenses minus all minimums
  4. Automate the attack — set up an automatic extra payment to your target debt on payday

💡 The Hybrid Approach

If your two smallest debts are also your highest-rate debts, the methods converge. In that case, use snowball order — you get the psychological win and the financial win simultaneously. Also: if your largest debt has an interest rate less than 5%, consider pausing aggressive paydown and investing in your TFSA instead — the investment return likely exceeds the debt cost.

The Debt-Free Date Calculator

Once you have your stack, calculate your debt-free date with this formula:

Months to Payoff = Balance ÷ (Monthly Extra Payment + Minimum Payment)

Example: $4,200 Visa at $84 minimum + $300 extra = $384/month → 4,200 ÷ 384 = 10.9 months to debt free on that card.

Once that card is gone, roll the full $384 to the next debt in your stack. This compounding payment is why people call it the "debt snowball" — the monthly attack payment grows with every elimination.

⚠️ The Minimum Payment Trap

On a $5,000 credit card at 22.99% with a $100 minimum payment, it takes over 8 years to pay off and costs $4,300 in interest alone — nearly doubling the original debt. The minimum payment is designed to keep you in debt. Even an extra $50/month cuts that timeline in half.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Avalanche saves the most money — attack highest interest rate debts first
  • ✦ Snowball builds the most momentum — attack smallest balance debts first
  • ✦ The method you stick to is always better than the perfect method you abandon
  • ✦ Rolling eliminated payments to the next debt accelerates your timeline exponentially
  • ✦ Minimum payments on credit cards can keep you in debt for a decade — always pay extra

Credit Card Traps — How Canadian Banks Keep You in Debt

Canadian credit cards carry some of the highest interest rates in the developed world. Here's exactly how the trap is designed — and the step-by-step escape plan.

⏱ ~7 min read
📘 Module 03
💳 Escape the Cycle

Canada's Big 5 banks charge credit card interest rates between 19.99% and 22.99% — among the highest in the developed world. In comparison, the US averages 20.75%, while many European countries cap rates at 10–12%.

This isn't accidental. The credit card product is engineered to maximize revolving balances. Understanding the design helps you defeat it.

How the Minimum Payment System Works Against You

Banks set minimum payments low — typically 2–3% of your balance or $10, whichever is greater. This keeps you feeling like you're managing your debt while ensuring maximum interest is collected.

The 5 Credit Card Traps to Avoid

Trap 1: The Balance Transfer Honeymoon

Banks offer 0% balance transfers for 6–12 months to pull balances from competitors. The trap: if you don't pay the full balance before the promotional period ends, the deferred interest hits all at once at the full rate (often 22.99%). Always set a calendar reminder 30 days before the promotional period expires.

Trap 2: The Cash Advance

Cash advances on credit cards carry no grace period — interest starts the moment you take the cash, not at the next billing cycle. Combined with typical cash advance rates of 22–28%, this is one of the most expensive borrowing options in Canada. Never use a credit card for cash unless it's a genuine emergency.

Trap 3: Retail Store Cards

Store credit cards (Canadian Tire, Bay, Sport Chek) often carry rates of 25–29.99%. They're pitched at checkout with a discount offer. The discount rarely justifies the cost of carrying even one month's balance at those rates.

Trap 4: Skipping a Payment

Many cards offer "skip a payment" options, marketed as a benefit. Skipping doesn't eliminate the payment — it adds to your balance and extends your debt timeline while interest continues accumulating. It's a product that benefits the bank, not you.

Trap 5: The Rewards Trap

Rewards cards justify higher annual fees and higher rates. Studies show that rewards card holders carry higher average balances — the psychological effect of "earning points" creates permission to spend more. If you're carrying a balance, the rewards are worth far less than the interest you're paying. Downgrade to a low-interest card (Scotiabank Value Visa at 12.99%, for example) until you're debt-free.

🍁 The Low-Interest Card Strategy

Canada has a class of low-interest credit cards capped at 8.99–12.99%. Scotiabank Value Visa, MBNA True Line, and National Bank MC Platinum are examples. If you're carrying a balance on a 22% card, transferring it to a 12.99% card while you pay it off saves significant interest — even with a balance transfer fee of 1–3%. Run the math for your specific balance before transferring.

How to Negotiate Your Interest Rate

Few Canadians know this: you can call your credit card provider and ask for a rate reduction. It works more often than you'd expect. Here's the script:

💡 The Rate Negotiation Script

"Hi, I've been a customer for [X] years and I have a good payment history. I've been offered a lower rate from [competitor]. I'd like to stay with you — can you reduce my rate to [10–15%]? If not, I may need to transfer my balance." You have the most leverage with: a long account history, no missed payments, and a competing offer (real or implied). Success rate for customers with good history: approximately 60–70%.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What You Just Learned

  • ✦ Canadian credit card rates (19–22.99%) are among the highest in the world — minimum payments are engineered to maximize bank profit
  • ✦ Balance transfers can save money, but always pay off before the promotional period ends
  • ✦ Cash advances charge interest immediately with no grace period — avoid them
  • ✦ Rewards cards cost more than they return if you carry a balance — switch to a low-interest card
  • ✦ Calling your bank to negotiate a rate reduction works ~65% of the time for good customers

Your Debt-Free Timeline — Build the Plan That Actually Works

Theory is worthless without execution. This lesson gives you the exact framework to map your personal debt-free date and build the habits that make it inevitable.

⏱ ~8 min read
📘 Module 03
📅 Build Your Plan

The difference between people who eliminate debt and those who don't isn't income. It isn't willpower. It's specificity. Vague goals produce vague results. A written, dated, automated debt-free plan produces results.

By the end of this lesson, you'll have one.

Step 2 — Find Your Attack Budget

Your attack budget is the money available to accelerate debt payoff beyond minimums:

Attack Budget = Take-Home Pay − Fixed Expenses − Variable Spending − All Debt Minimums

Even $100–$300/month makes a dramatic difference. On the example above, an extra $200/month attack budget eliminates the $4,200 Visa in 15 months instead of 5+ years on minimums alone.

Step 3 — The Acceleration Tactics

Beyond your regular attack budget, use these Canadian-specific strategies to inject lump sums into your debt payoff:

  • Tax refund — the average Canadian gets ~$1,800 back. Direct 100% to your #1 priority debt
  • GST/HST credit — quarterly payments of $150–$400 depending on income. Redirect to debt
  • Canada Child Benefit (CCB) — if you have children, consider allocating a portion to debt payoff
  • Work bonus or raise — commit 50% of any income increase to debt before lifestyle inflation sets in
  • Side hustle income — Module 05 covers this in depth, but even $300/month from gig work adds $3,600/year to your debt attack
  • Selling unused items — Facebook Marketplace, Kijiji, and eBay can generate $500–$2,000 quickly

Step 4 — The Automated System

Automation removes willpower from the equation. Set up:

  1. Auto-pay minimums on all debts — this protects your credit score and prevents late fees
  2. Auto-transfer your attack budget to a separate "debt attack" account on payday
  3. Weekly manual payment from that account to your #1 priority debt — making weekly payments reduces average daily balance and cuts interest slightly
🍁 The Canadian Debt-Free Milestone

Becoming debt-free (excluding mortgage) fundamentally changes your monthly cash flow. The average Canadian carrying $33,000 in consumer debt has $525/month in minimum payments. Eliminating that debt frees up $525/month — $6,300/year — to redirect into TFSA and RRSP investments. At 7% annual growth, that $6,300/year becomes over $280,000 in 20 years. Debt freedom isn't just about being debt-free — it's about unleashing your wealth-building capacity.

The Psychological Side of Debt Payoff

Paying off debt is a marathon. These evidence-based strategies maintain momentum:

  • Track visually — a simple progress bar or chart showing your balance dropping is powerful motivation
  • Celebrate milestones — every $1,000 paid off deserves a small reward (not on a credit card)
  • Tell one person — accountability partners increase follow-through rates significantly
  • Review monthly — recalculate your debt-free date monthly to see it getting closer
  • Don't add new debt — cut cards if necessary, use a debit card, delete saved payment info online
📊 The Debt-Free Transformation

Studies of debt payoff programs show that Canadians who use a written plan with automated payments are 3x more likely to complete debt payoff versus those who manage it manually. The plan removes the daily decision — you don't have to choose to pay down debt because it's already automated. The choice was made once, in advance, when you set it up.
